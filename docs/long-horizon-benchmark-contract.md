# BEAM long-horizon benchmark contract

## 1. What BEAM evaluates

Long-horizon tasks are persistent-goal tasks in a bounded environment. An agent
must carry progress, decisions, intermediate results, and constraints across
hundreds to thousands of causally dependent effective transitions until the
goal is complete. The defining difficulty is not raw prompt length. It is that
an early task state can remain behaviorally relevant after many context resets,
can be superseded by a later state, and can interact with local plans and
higher-authority constraints.

For BEAM, a trajectory is long-horizon only when all four conditions hold:

1. **goal persistence**: the transitions belong to one bounded task and one
   continuing global objective rather than a collection of unrelated queries;
2. **pre-decision causal span**: the reported steps form a replayable dependency
   chain before the scored decision, each counted step produces a distinct
   semantic task effect consumed downstream, and repeated text, digest-only
   links, trailing work, or post-hoc padding do not count;
3. **delayed state dependence**: a later executable action depends on progress,
   decisions, results, or constraints originating before one or more context
   resets;
4. **persistent-channel competition**: the agent may recover that state from
   workspace artifacts, task memory, full history, or some combination, and the
   evaluator measures those channels separately.

BEAM is therefore a benchmark of **memory-supported state maintenance and
behavioral control at critical continuation decisions**. It is not a benchmark
of isolated factual recall, and it is not a claim that the current release
executes every one of hundreds or thousands of microsteps with an LLM policy.
Each episode is a replayable persistent-task trajectory; the evaluator samples
decision points where long-range state should change the next executable action.

The operational horizon has four measured axes:

1. temporal reach: session handoffs, state age, and event-to-decision distance;
2. causal reach: dependency depth and the number of relevant state transitions;
3. state dynamics: replacement, revocation, invalidation, scope, and authority;
4. environmental redundancy: whether required state is explicit, derivable, or
   absent in the current workspace.

Token count is an auxiliary cost variable, not the definition of horizon.

### 1.1 Long-horizon construct-validity contract

BEAM separates the *task horizon* from the *evaluation sampling rate*. A task
may contain many environment and agent transitions while only a smaller set of
causally consequential decisions is sent to an expensive policy model. This is
valid only when the intervening trajectory is replayable and affects later
state, dependencies, workspace artifacts, or available actions; duplicated
text, filler events, and repeated retrieval calls do not count as task steps.

A release that claims the full hundreds-to-thousands-step setting must report:

- the number of effective agent/environment transitions, excluding padding;
- the number of session handoffs and policy-controlled decisions;
- the distribution of state-to-decision age and event distance;
- dependency depth and the number of state transitions upstream of each SCEU;
- the minimum and maximum number of effective causal ancestors of scored
  decisions, semantic-effect coverage, and consumed-prefix-effect coverage;
- which trajectory segments were executed by the policy, replayed from a
  frozen trace, or generated by the environment.

BEAM assigns every episode one machine-audited trajectory interaction tier:

- `replay_backed_critical_decision`: the scored policy decision follows an
  effective causal prefix, but no scored decision is an ancestor of a later
  scored decision;
- `sparse_closed_loop`: a scored decision conditions a later effective step
  and a later scored decision, but the policy-evaluated span remains below the
  full long-horizon threshold;
- `online_long_horizon_agent_execution`: at least 200 policy-evaluated steps
  are causally linked through the declared trajectory.

The current v0.11/v0.12/v0.13 releases are intentionally in the first tier. Their
claim is delayed task-state control at preregistered critical decisions after
audited long-horizon prefixes, not full online multi-hundred-step Agent
execution. This interaction tier is included in the frozen task-span artifact,
design audit, report summary, and contribution-evidence contract.

BEAM v0.11 operationalizes the lower end of this definition as at least 200
effective causal ancestors of a scored decision. Its 16-session Software
triplets contain at least 256 public prefix transitions plus a separately
counted continuation decision. The
matched decision occurs only after the final session prefix and 15 handoffs, so
no post-decision work is used to inflate its reported horizon. Each transition
records its execution mode, state/workspace references, predecessor step IDs,
predecessor effect digests, its own reproducible effect digest, a unique
semantic effect, and the predecessor effects it consumes. The loader rejects
duplicate effects or semantic dependencies that do not match the declared DAG.
The threshold is computed from the scored decision's causal ancestors, not the
episode total. The entire effect DAG is verified during plan loading and
regeneration. These are frozen
task-trajectory transitions, not 256 independent online policy calls; the
policy is evaluated sparsely at preregistered continuation points.

v0.13 uses the same 256-step public prefix but samples 13 decisions along the
trajectory to identify C2's temporal risk sets. It therefore records 269
effective steps in total, a maximum decision causal span of 256, and 13
policy-evaluated critical decisions. Those decisions do not condition later
prefix state in this release, so the interaction tier remains replay-backed;
they are also repeated observations within one episode, not 13 independent
statistical units.

This boundary is substantive: long-range causal dependence cannot be inferred
from a long prompt, a large token count, or repeated filler events. The main
v0.10 ranking release retains the earlier critical-decision trajectory and
must not independently be used to support the literal 200-step claim.

### 1.2 Same-decision horizon-dose diagnostic

A long trajectory alone does not identify a long-horizon effect: the same state
conflict might already be difficult after a short delay. BEAM therefore defines
a supplementary horizon-dose panel that keeps the terminal task fixed while
increasing only the preregistered joint dose of effective transitions and
session handoffs:

| Dose | Sessions | Handoffs | Total effective steps | Terminal decision causal ancestors | Interpretation |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| short control | 4 | 3 | 65 | 64 | non-long-horizon reference |
| medium | 8 | 7 | 129 | 128 | intermediate dose |
| long | 16 | 15 | 257 | 256 | meets BEAM's current ≥200-step floor |

For every static, state-evolution, and hierarchical-conflict history, the panel
holds fixed the terminal request, current authoritative state semantics,
workspace semantics and recoverability, action catalog, correct action, opaque
option mapping, continuation scope, hidden checker, and package. The target is
always after the final prefix. A machine audit rejects a panel when any of
these invariants changes.

The terminal contract is complete only when its required closure contains
every current state that can make any offered action valid or invalid. Stable
replacement, revocation, authority, and scope rules are shared task semantics
for all conditions; oracle receives a minimal current-state realization but no
condition-specific decision rule. This prevents incomplete gold state or an
oracle-only instruction from being mistaken for a memory-system failure.

This panel identifies a **joint horizon dose**, not an isolated handoff effect:
transition count, dependency depth, and handoffs increase together. A future
factorial panel can separate those axes. The v0.12 release
`software-matched-horizon-panels-v0.12.0` now freezes, verifies, regenerates,
plans, reports, and validates this design end to end. Until it is run with a
policy/backend matrix, that remains structural and measurement-contract
evidence, not empirical evidence that performance degrades with horizon.

One panel contains nine dependent physical members but contributes one
statistical unit. The report therefore suppresses generic physical-episode
inference and within-panel triplet inference. Its dedicated
`horizon_panel_statistics.json` first reduces the grid to one effect per panel,
then bootstraps and sign-flips panels. This routing is validated as part of the
hashed artifact contract.

## 2. The paper's central claim

Existing benchmarks can show that a memory system answers questions from a long
history or changes downstream task success. BEAM asks a narrower and more
diagnostic question:

> When an agent resumes a persistent task, does its memory system preserve the
> current, authoritative task state and causally support the correct next action
> beyond what the workspace already provides?

This claim is stronger than recall accuracy but narrower than general autonomous
agent competence. The comparison target is a memory system attached to a fixed
policy and executable task environment.

The scientific object is therefore not “memory accuracy at a larger context
length.” It is the integrity of a delayed state-control channel:

```text
past task events -> native writes/updates -> retrieval -> model-visible state
                 -> executable continuation -> future task state
                         ^
                         | workspace is a measured competing channel
```

Long-horizon constructs intervene on the upstream history while the downstream
decision contract is held fixed. Lifecycle instrumentation then localizes the
broken link. Drift measures the longitudinal behavioral consequence. These are
one identification design, not three independent benchmark features.

## 3. Contributions and evidence contracts

### C1. Counterfactually identified, workspace-aware state-control benchmark

The dataset is generated from latent state and state-transition events before
surface dialogue or questions are rendered. The evaluation unit is a
State-Conditioned Evaluation Unit (SCEU): one checkpoint, one current-state
closure, one workspace snapshot, one opaque action catalog, and one
programmatically checked continuation.

This design changes what is being tested:

- state is versioned and can be replaced, revoked, invalidated, or scoped;
- the output is an executable continuation, not a factual answer;
- memory is written in each system's native representation;
- the workspace is a first-class competing information channel;
- workspace-only, full-context, and oracle-current-state controls separate
  memory value from task solvability and context availability.

Primary evidence:

- behavior correctness and executable checker score;
- gain beyond workspace-only;
- gap to full context and oracle-current-state;
- oracle-gap closure;
- current-state and authority-conflict resolution;
- results stratified by handoff, state age, dependency depth, transition count,
  construct kind, and workspace recoverability.

The supplementary v0.11 mechanism release adds a counterfactually matched
triplet at one final-session decision. Within a group, the terminal request,
checkpoint, action catalog, valid action, opaque option mapping, continuation
scope, checker-relevant terminal predicates, and prefix/workspace shape are
held fixed while the preceding history is changed between `static`,
`evolution`, and `hierarchical_conflict`. Across groups, three terminal
archetypes rotate the correct action between current-v2 offline, current-v1
offline, and a valid scoped hosted exception. Opaque option positions are
balanced at the same time. Thus neither an always-safe action nor a fixed
option position can solve the mechanism split. The release reports evolution
and conflict penalties relative to the static member of the same group. This
is the primary identification test that BEAM measures more than static recall
under a longer transcript.

The primary workspace-adjusted mechanism estimand is a difference-in-
differences. For memory condition $m$, history construct $h$, and its matched
static member $s$, define the marginal value beyond workspace as
$G_{m,h}=Y_{m,h}-Y_{workspace,h}$. BEAM reports
$G_{m,h}-G_{m,s}$, equivalently the negative of the construct-penalty excess
over workspace. This subtraction matters because an evolved history can also
change what is recoverable from files and logs. A raw evolution penalty alone
cannot identify a memory-channel failure; the workspace-adjusted penalty can.
Oracle invariance is the solvability check, while full-context invariance is a
diagnostic check that the terminal contract itself did not become harder.
Both are enforced as per-policy post-run gates over every matched group and all
three history variants. If oracle fails, solvability has not been established;
if full context fails, the policy's ability to interpret complete history is a
remaining confound. In either case, the corresponding report cannot promote a
memory-system deficit to an identified memory-channel effect.

The supplementary horizon-dose estimand asks whether that same construct
penalty is amplified by causal distance. Let
$P_{m,h,l}=Y_{m,static,l}-Y_{m,h,l}$ be the history penalty for memory condition
$m$, construct $h$, and horizon dose $l$, and let $w$ denote workspace-only.
Then:

$$
A_{m,h}=\left(P_{m,h,long}-P_{w,h,long}\right)
       -\left(P_{m,h,short}-P_{w,h,short}\right).
$$

A positive $A_{m,h}$ means the memory channel suffers additional evolution or
authority-conflict degradation at the long dose, beyond both workspace-only
and the same construct at the short control. A null or negative result is
scientifically meaningful and would prevent BEAM from claiming that the
observed mechanism failure is specifically amplified by horizon.

### C2. Goal-relative long-horizon behavioral drift

BEAM does not claim that behavioral drift as a general idea is new. It provides
a state-grounded operationalization for ordinary persistent-task evolution,
rather than defining drift only through adversarial memory poisoning or generic
output change.

A drift-compatible violation is a checked action that conflicts with the
episode's current authoritative state:

- `constraint_loss`: a persistent constraint no longer controls behavior;
- `plan_deviation`: the chosen continuation departs from the currently valid
  plan, including premature adoption of a future plan;
- `stale_state`: a revoked or superseded state still controls behavior;
- `local_over_global`: a local subgoal or scoped exception overrides a global
  goal or higher-authority constraint.

BEAM does not silently equate such a violation with longitudinal drift. An
observed drift event requires adherence to the same category **and the same
state lineage** at an earlier distinct eligible checkpoint, followed by a later
violation of that lineage. Adherence to one constraint cannot anchor failure of
another plan. A first-observation error remains a violation but has no identified
drift onset. Primary evidence
therefore separates category-eligible violation rate from adherence-anchored
first-drift handoff, drift-free survival, persistence across distinct
checkpoints, and recovery after a valid update or fresh reminder. Trajectories
without an adherence anchor are excluded from the longitudinal denominator
rather than filled with zero. Category-only legacy trajectories are descriptive
and cannot satisfy the C2 evidence gate. Oracle-current-state and full-context
must cover the same lineages without an adherence-to-violation transition before
drift can be attributed specifically to the memory channel. The episode is the
statistical unit; SCEUs and lineages remain within-episode observations.

Each SCEU/category must expose one focal state lineage. If one continuation
would make the same drift category simultaneously refer to multiple lineages,
the generator must split it into separate SCEUs; report generation fails closed
rather than broadcasting one observed category flag to several states.

The matched v0.11 triplet contains one common terminal decision per physical
member. It identifies a construct-specific *drift-compatible violation excess*
relative to matched static history; it does not by itself identify longitudinal
onset. Onset and survival require the multi-checkpoint trajectory release.

The separate v0.13 release supplies that temporal design. Each episode has 13
registered decisions over shared constraint and plan lineages. For every
canonical drift category, the frozen evaluator must contain three distinct
ordered checkpoints: an adherence-capable anchor, a later ordinary challenge,
and a still-later valid-update or fresh-reminder recovery opportunity. The final
recovery decision closes the stale-state and local-over-global windows that are
missing from the 12-decision v0.10 trajectory. Removing that decision makes the
pre-call C2 recovery audit fail. This structural eligibility does not assert
that a tested system actually adhered, drifted, or recovered; those are
post-run observations with oracle/full-context control gates.

### C3. Decision-aligned memory-to-behavior attribution

For the same SCEU, let `R_d` be current required state and let `M_d` be the
subset not explicitly recoverable from workspace. BEAM reconstructs:

```text
stored -> backend-retrieved -> model-visible -> intervention evidence -> behavior
```

The earliest supported failure is assigned on that fixed decision:

- required state absent from an observed store: storage failure;
- stored state absent from backend retrieval: retrieval failure;
- retrieved state removed before the model prompt: exposure failure;
- all required state visible but the checked action is wrong: utilization or
  decision failure.

The decision layer is then subtyped by intervention evidence. If every visible
required state was probed and no stable unique effect was detected, the result is
`visible_without_detected_unique_causal_effect`. This label does not exclude
redundant or compensated use. If a targeted intervention changes the action
but the action remains wrong, it is
`visible_causally_influential_but_wrong`. This indicates misapplication or a later
reasoning failure, not non-use. Insufficient probe coverage remains
`visible_use_evidence_incomplete`. BEAM therefore distinguishes the user's
“the model saw it but did not use it” case only as a conservative
no-detected-unique-effect statement; it does not claim direct access to
attention or an internal cognitive process.

If storage evidence is unavailable, the result is explicitly
`storage_evidence_unavailable`; it is never counted as a storage failure.
Lifecycle provenance (`native/exact` versus inventory-inferred) and semantic
state alignment are reported separately.

For successful behavior, visibility alone does not prove use. A memory is
labelled causally influential only when a repeat-stable, state-targeted
replacement or leave-one-out intervention changes the action or checker result.
Thus identified unique causal influence is a lower bound. A correct decision
without detected intervention effect is reported as success without a detected
unique causal effect, not as proof that memory was unused.

Primary evidence:

- write coverage/selectivity and current-state storage precision/recall/F1;
- stale retention, update/delete responsiveness, and write-to-continuation
  alignment;
- stored-to-retrieved and retrieved-to-visible conditional yields;
- visible-state probe coverage and probed-state causal-use rate;
- first-failure distribution and decision-layer use/misuse subtype, with exact
  and inferred provenance separated.

BEAM also reports an outcome-equivalent fault-profile diagnostic. For two
memory conditions $a$ and $b$ aligned at the same policy, readout, episode,
SCEU, opportunity, checkpoint, current-state contract, selected action, and
correctness label, let $F_a$ and $F_b$ be their earliest supported stage plus
the intervention-grounded utilization subtype when applicable. Then:

$$
D_{OE}=\frac{\sum_{(a,b)\in OE}\mathbb{1}[F_a\ne F_b]}{|OE|}.
$$

A positive $D_{OE}$ shows that an end-task outcome collapses distinct observed
repair targets. It does not claim that stage diagnosis itself is new, and it
does not turn condition pairs from one decision into independent samples. A
zero estimate is a valid result, not evidence that two systems are equivalent.

### 3.4 One identification design, not three disconnected features

The contributions should be presented as one experimental identification
design. State-first generation provides the time-indexed evaluator truth;
workspace controls identify whether memory adds information beyond persistent
artifacts; executable continuations turn state errors into behavioral
consequences; lifecycle traces and targeted interventions then localize where
that consequence arose. Removing any component breaks the inference:

- without evolving state, the benchmark reduces to recall;
- without workspace controls, apparent memory gains may come from information
  already available in files or logs;
- without executable continuations, factual correctness need not imply correct
  task behavior;
- without same-decision traces, storage, retrieval, exposure, and use remain
  confounded;
- without interventions, visibility cannot be interpreted as causal use.
- without outcome-equivalent pairing, aggregate accuracy cannot demonstrate
  whether apparently identical behavior arose through different failure paths.

The resulting causal question is not “does a longer memory benchmark lower
accuracy?” It is: **holding the terminal decision contract fixed, how much
additional behavioral control is lost when the history requires state update
or authority resolution, after subtracting what the workspace alone can
support, and at which observed memory stage does that loss first appear?**

### 3.5 Executable contribution-evidence contract

Each complete qualification report emits a report-level
`contribution_evidence.json` plus a human-readable Markdown companion. For C1,
C2, and C3, the artifact records the applicable claim scope, required controls
and readiness gates, required and observed estimands, evidence counts, source
artifacts, and an explicit claim boundary. The validator reconstructs this
artifact from the underlying report and rejects a hand-edited or internally
inconsistent claim status.

For C2, schema-v6 validation additionally requires a `state_lineage_id`,
lineage evidence mode, and lineage-backed flag for every trajectory. Readiness
requires repeated-checkpoint coverage, prior adherence on that same lineage,
and clean oracle-current-state/full-context trajectories. A category-only legacy
row can remain in a report for descriptive compatibility, but cannot make C2
`ready`.

The artifact is report-level rather than episode-level. C1's matched mechanism
estimand is defined across the three members of a counterfactual group, so an
episode-local “contribution passed” label would treat dependent physical
members as independent evidence. Likewise, `ready` means only that the
measurement contract is complete. Effect direction, uncertainty, and
confirmatory status remain properties of the pre-specified statistical
analysis, not of the readiness label.

There are two separate gates. First, a policy-free
`experiment_design_audit.json` (with a human-readable Markdown companion) is
computed before any native writer or policy
call and bound into the immutable run identity. It checks whether the frozen
release can identify the claimed construct at all: complete invariant
triplets, balanced terminal actions and opaque positions, balanced workspace
recoverability and terminal archetypes, checker-positive/negative drift
controls, memory-reliant decisions, exclusion of future state, and verified
effective-step effect chains. A v0.13 design must additionally pass category-
complete same-lineage anchor/challenge/recovery gates and a current action-
relevant intervention-target gate for every memory-reliant SCEU. Second,
post-run measurement gates check whether
the systems and controls actually produced usable evidence. Passing the first
cannot substitute for the second, and neither establishes a positive or
statistically significant effect.

The pre-call audit also contains machine-validated, contribution-specific
analysis contracts for C1--C3. For matched C1, it fixes
`counterfactual_group` as the analysis and uncertainty unit; `static` as the
reference; evolution and hierarchical conflict as the treatments;
workspace-only as the adjustment control; and
`state_evolution_penalty_excess_over_workspace` plus
`hierarchical_conflict_penalty_excess_over_workspace` as the two primary
estimands. Raw history penalties and correctness penalties are secondary. The
contract freezes full context as the history-availability control and oracle
current state as the terminal-solvability control; their per-policy matched
gates must pass before C1 is evidence-ready.

The C2 contract freezes the temporal claim supported by the release. Matched
and horizon releases support only construct-specific endpoint
drift-compatible violation excess; they cannot be relabelled as longitudinal
onset. The v0.13 longitudinal release may estimate onset, survival,
persistence, and recovery only when its pre-call lineage/recovery design gates
and post-run prior-adherence and clean-control gates pass. The C3 contract
freezes the SCEU as its unit, the ordered
storage → retrieval → model-exposure → intervention → behavior trace,
exact-versus-inferred provenance reporting, and repeat-stable neutral
replacement as the primary unique-effect intervention. No detected unique
effect is a lower-bound diagnostic, not proof that the model internally ignored
the memory.

Holm correction is applied within each registered estimand across
policy/condition/readout cells. The validator rejects a report that relabels
these roles, expands endpoint evidence into onset, or turns a C3 observational
stage label into causal use, even if its file hash is updated. This
content-addressed pre-call specification is not represented as an external
public preregistration.

The immutable plan also records an explicit `analysis_phase`. `development`
and `diagnostic` runs have no inferential sample-size implication;
`calibration` requires at least 3 matched counterfactual groups (or 5 standard
or longitudinal episodes), and `confirmatory` requires at least 30 matched
groups (or 50 standard or longitudinal episodes). The counterfactual group,
not each triplet member, is the unit counted; for v0.13, one episode counts once,
not once per repeated decision. This rule prevents a calibration run from being relabelled after
results are seen. Both the planner and the report validator apply the same
shared contract, including when a report is produced outside the canonical
wrapper. It establishes only phase-label and scale eligibility:
disjoint data, a frozen analysis contract, passed measurement gates, effect
estimates, and uncertainty remain independently necessary.

## 4. Research questions and falsifiable evidence

| Research question | Primary comparison | Required evidence | What would falsify the intended claim |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **RQ1: What behavioral control does task memory add beyond workspace, and is the identified state-control difficulty amplified by horizon?** | Each memory condition versus workspace-only on the same SCEU; within-group static/evolution/conflict difference-in-differences; same-decision short/medium/long dose, with full-context and oracle-current-state as diagnostic bounds | paired gain beyond workspace, workspace-adjusted construct penalty, horizon amplification, oracle-gap closure, and stratification by state age, dependency depth, transition count, and workspace recoverability | no gain on workspace-absent decisions, oracle/full-context also fail the matched terminal contract, the construct effect disappears after subtracting workspace-only, or the long-dose effect is indistinguishable from the short control |
| **RQ2: When and how does memory-supported behavior drift from the authoritative task state?** | state-lineage/category-eligible decisions over episode time, plus matched workspace/oracle/full-context trajectories | violation prevalence separated from same-lineage adherence-anchored onset, survival, persistence, and update/reminder recovery; matched endpoint violation excess | drift labels do not track programmatic state validity, same-lineage adherence anchors are absent, or oracle/full-context behavior drifts on the same lineages |
| **RQ3: Where in the memory-to-action channel does failure first become identifiable, and can the same checked outcome conceal different failures?** | the same decision and required-state set across native store, backend retrieval, model input, intervention, and checker result; outcome-equivalent pairs across memory conditions | conditional stage yields, earliest-supported-failure distribution, provenance coverage, detected unique-causal-effect lower bound, and outcome-equivalent fault-profile divergence | lifecycle evidence is missing/ambiguous, stages are compared on different decisions, intervention results are unstable, or no aligned outcome-equivalent pair exists |

These RQs deliberately avoid a generic “which system recalls more?” headline.
The comparative result is useful only after the benchmark has shown that the
task is solvable, the workspace baseline is insufficient on designated SCEUs,
and the attribution instrumentation is stable.

## 5. Why this is not “MemBench at a longer context”

The differentiator is the evaluation intervention, not the number of tokens:

| Dimension | Recall-oriented memory benchmark | BEAM |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gold unit | question and answer | current state closure and executable action |
| World | mostly historical evidence | replayable state with valid/invalid versions |
| Agent output | fact or explanation | checked task continuation |
| External state | usually folded into history | workspace measured as a separate channel |
| Memory write | often benchmark chunks/history | system-native write lifecycle |
| Error diagnosis | answer/retrieval error | same-decision storage/retrieval/exposure/utilization |
| Same final outcome | treated as the same result | tested for divergent observed failure and repair profiles |
| Temporal failure | update/forgetting accuracy | goal-relative drift onset, persistence, and recovery |
| Causal evidence | correlation with retrieved text | targeted memory-object intervention |
| Construct identification | harder items compared with easier items | static/evolution/conflict histories counterfactually matched at the same terminal decision |
| Horizon specificity | a long transcript is assumed to be a long-horizon test | the same terminal state, workspace, options, and checker are compared at short/medium/long pre-decision causal doses |
| Workspace confounding | external artifacts folded into the prompt/history | construct penalty is explicitly adjusted by the matched workspace-only penalty |
| Shortcut resistance | often an unchecked dataset property | balanced terminal gold actions and opaque positions with enforced always-action/always-option gates |
| Horizon accounting | prompt length or event count | pre-decision effective-step DAG, execution-mode provenance, and final-session handoff audit |

Accordingly, the paper should avoid “the first lifecycle benchmark” and “the
first drift benchmark.” Recent work already evaluates incremental memory
competencies, dynamic state, arbitrary-length agent trajectories, task-level
memory benefit, memory lifecycle modules, and adversarial memory drift. BEAM's
defensible novelty is the joint, auditable design: **evolving task state +
workspace control + executable continuation + decision-aligned causal fault
localization**.

Relevant adjacent work includes
[LongMemEval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.10813),
[MemoryAgentBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.05257),
[Mem2ActBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.19935),
[AMA-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.22769),
[LongMemEval-V2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12493),
[MemoryArena](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.16313),
[AgingBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26302),
[MEME](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12477),
[WorldMemArena](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29341),
[STATE-Bench](https://opensource.microsoft.com/blog/2026/05/19/introducing-state-bench-a-benchmark-for-ai-agent-memory/),
[MemEvoBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15774), and the
[agent-memory systems characterization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06448),
[Remember When It Matters](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08716), and
[PM-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12385).

MemoryArena already couples memory acquisition with multi-session actions;
MEME already covers evolving multi-entity state and dependency reasoning; and
WorldMemArena already evaluates writing, maintenance, retrieval, and use as a
memory lifecycle. BEAM therefore does not claim any of those components in
isolation. Its narrower distinction is that workspace is an explicit competing
information channel, history structure is counterfactually manipulated while
the terminal executable decision is fixed, and model-visible memory objects
are intervened on at that same decision to obtain a conservative unique
causal-effect lower bound and earliest supported failure stage.

AgingBench already uses temporal dependency graphs and paired probes to
diagnose write, retrieval, and utilization failures, including cases where the
same wrong answer implies different repairs. Remember When It Matters already
names behavioral state decay in long-horizon agents, while Mem2ActBench and
MemoryArena already connect remembered information to actions. Accordingly,
BEAM does not claim the first action-oriented memory benchmark, the first stage
diagnosis, or the discovery of behavioral decay. The defensible increment is
the joint identification contract: counterfactually matched state histories,
a separately measured workspace path, executable state-validity checks,
goal-relative longitudinal drift, and native-trace/intervention attribution at
one fixed decision.

## 6. Claim boundary for the current experiments

The completed five-episode v0.8.0 GPT-only calibration establishes that the
end-to-end native system pipeline, lifecycle traces, interventions,
programmatic checker, and reporting artifacts execute. A confirmatory backend
ranking requires more evidence because this run still fails
oracle and sham-stability gates, and it samples critical decisions over 16
sessions rather than executing hundreds or thousands of mutually dependent
policy-controlled steps online.

The frozen v0.8 task contract also explains part of the control contamination:
the local-conflict SCEUs omitted current plan/update predicates from the
action-relevant closure, and authority/scope precedence was not yet a stable
task rule shared by every condition. Those control errors therefore cannot be
interpreted as memory-system drift. The frozen artifacts are not rewritten;
new releases fail before model calls when the current-action state closure is
incomplete and expose the same governance semantics to all conditions.

An integrity-verified, zero-API post-hoc reanalysis joins that report to the
exact frozen evaluator dataset declared in its manifest. Across 600 observed
decisions, 154 were memory-reliant under the workspace-absent definition. The
earliest supported stage included 13 storage, 10 retrieval, 26 exposure, and
24 utilization failures. Among 166 outcome-equivalent aligned pairs, 56 had
different observed fault profiles. The same reanalysis reconstructed 200
evaluator-derived state-lineage trajectories; 194 had an adherence-anchored
follow-up window, 34 category-lineage trajectories showed observed drift at 20
unique onset decisions, and 14 of 18 recovery-evaluable trajectories recovered.
However, oracle-current-state and full-context each showed two drift
trajectories in `constraint_loss`/`local_over_global`. The run therefore supports
descriptive C2 trajectories and the C3 claim that endpoint-equivalent behavior
can hide different repair targets, but not a global memory-specific drift
effect. The analysis is post-hoc, rows and pairs are dependent within
episodes/SCEUs, and five episodes cannot support a population rate or backend
ranking.

The v0.11 matched generator, task-step effect chain, freeze/verify/regen path,
construct penalties, and readiness gates are implemented and locally tested.
Any completed v0.11 server run is a mechanism experiment, whereas v0.10 is a
trajectory calibration/ranking stream; their physical episodes and estimands
must never be pooled into a single backend ranking.

The same-decision horizon-dose generator and structural invariant audit are a
supplementary diagnostic. Any completed v0.12 run must use its frozen panel-
level estimand and interaction-tier audit; it cannot be retrofitted into the
v0.10/v0.11 ranking or described as full online Agent execution.

Therefore:

- use the calibration for debugging, qualitative examples, and measurement
  validation;
- use regenerated long-horizon construct and failure-attribution reports as
  descriptive analysis;
- reserve comparative performance and drift conclusions for the frozen,
  disjoint confirmatory split after every measurement gate passes;
- describe BEAM as a critical-decision benchmark for memory systems **within**
  long-horizon tasks, not as a complete benchmark of every long-horizon agent
  capability.

## 7. Recommended abstract-level contribution wording

> We introduce BEAM, a state-first and workspace-aware benchmark for diagnosing
> how memory systems support critical continuation decisions in persistent
> tasks. BEAM generates replayable trajectories with versioned goals,
> constraints, plans, scoped authority, and executable consequences, and
> counterfactually varies static, evolving, and authority-conflicting histories
> while holding the terminal decision contract fixed. A matched horizon-dose
> panel further varies the pre-decision causal span while preserving the same
> terminal state, workspace semantics, options, and checker. BEAM measures
> memory's marginal behavioral value beyond the matched workspace-only channel. It
> further operationalizes goal-relative behavioral drift across state updates
> and handoffs, including counterfactually matched static, state-evolution, and
> authority-conflict histories at the same terminal decision. Finally, BEAM
> aligns native memory lifecycle traces and targeted
> interventions at the same decision to localize failures to storage, retrieval,
> model exposure, or behavioral utilization, while preserving an explicit
> unobservable category when the evidence does not support attribution. BEAM
> further quantifies when identical checked actions conceal different observed
> failure profiles, without treating those dependent pairs as new statistical
> samples.
